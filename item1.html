Autumn Crocus

The Autumn Crocus, less commonly known as Colchicum Autumnale is a toxic autumn-blooming flowering plant that resembles true crocuses, but is a member of the plant family Colchicaceae, unlike true crocuses which belong to the family Iridaceae. The bulb-like corms of the autumn crocus contain colchicine, which is approved in many countries to treat gout, and mediterranean fever.

Distribution

Colchicum autumnale is the only species of its family native to Great Britain, and Ireland, with notable populations under the stewardship of the County Wildlife Trusts. It also occurs across mainland Europe from Portugal to Ukraine It is reportedly naturalized in Denmark, Sweden, European Russia, the Baltic states, and New Zealand.





Toxicity

Colchicum plants are deadly poisonous due to their colchicine content, and have been mistaken by foragers for ramsons, which they vaguely resemble. The symptoms of colchicine poisoning resemble those of arsenic, and there is no known antidote. The plants pose particular threat to mamals, as the leaves, and fruit contain the highest levels of toxin, but all parts are regarded as poisonous.
